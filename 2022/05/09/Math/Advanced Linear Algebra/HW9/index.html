1.9.1\(\ \small \mbox{Kronecker product?}\)

Consider two linear maps \(X: V \rightarrow V\) and \(Y: W \rightarrow W\) over finite dimensional spaces. We define \(X \otimes Y\) to be the map from \(V \times W\) to \(V \otimes W\), such that \((\boldsymbol{v}, \boldsymbol{w}) \mapsto X \boldsymbol{v} \otimes Y \boldsymbol{w}\). 1. Verify that \(X \otimes Y\) is bilinear. Therefore we can think of it as a linear map \(X \otimes Y: V \otimes W \rightarrow V \otimes W\) 2. Show that trace \(X \otimes Y=(\operatorname{trace} X)(\operatorname{trace} Y)\).

\((1)\)

1.9.2\(\ \small \mbox{Trace as a tensor}\)

Note that a linear map from \(\mathbb{R}^{n} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{n}\) is an element of \(\mathbb{R}^{n} \otimes\left(\mathbb{R}^{n}\right)^{*}\). Hence \(\mathbb{R}^{n} \otimes\left(\mathbb{R}^{n}\right)^{*}\) is exactly the space of \(n \times n\) matrices.

The trace map send matrices to numbers. So we have \(\operatorname{trace}: \mathbb{R}^{n} \otimes\left(\mathbb{R}^{n}\right)^{*} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\). It is also linear. So what space is trace an element of? What are the entries of the tensor trace?

\((1)\)

1.9.3\(\ \small \mbox{Quantum Entanglement}\)

(Optional Background) Quantum physics usually start like this. Given a particual A, its "possible states" are unit vectors \(\boldsymbol{v}\) in some (usually infinite dimensional) inner product space \(H_{A}\). (We use unit vectors, because \(\|\boldsymbol{v}\|\) usually means total probability, which should be one.) When we make an observation on \(A\), the observation is encoded by a (usually self-adjoint) linear map \(L: H_{A} \rightarrow H_{A}\). If \(L=I\) the identity map, then this means we do not observe at all.]

For simplicity, suppose we have two inner product spaces \(H_{A}, H_{B}\). (You may think that they describe the states of two different particles \(A\) and \(B\).) Then on their tensor space \(H_{A} \otimes H_{B}\), we define \(\left(\boldsymbol{v}_{1} \otimes \boldsymbol{w}_{1}, \boldsymbol{v}_{2} \otimes \boldsymbol{w}_{2}\right)=\) \(\left(\boldsymbol{v}_{1}, \boldsymbol{v}_{2}\right)\left(\boldsymbol{w}_{1}, \boldsymbol{w}_{2}\right)\) for rank one elements, and extend this bilinearly on all elements of \(H_{A} \otimes H_{B}\). For simplicity, we assume that \(H_{A}=H_{B}=\mathbb{R}^{2}\).

  1. Show that \((-,-)\) on \(H_{A} \otimes H_{B}\) is indeed an inner product. (You don't need to prove bilinearity, but prove that it is symmetric and positive definite.)
  2. Show that a \(\boldsymbol{e}_{1} \otimes \boldsymbol{e}_{1}+b \boldsymbol{e}_{2} \otimes \boldsymbol{e}_{2}\) has rank two when a,b are both non-zero. (Rank one states in \(H_{A} \otimes H_{B}\) are called non-entangled states. And these rank two states here are entangled states.)
  3. Given a pair of linear maps \(L_{A}: H_{A} \rightarrow H_{A}\) and \(L_{B}: H_{B} \rightarrow H_{B}\), we define the expected observation result of \(L_{A} \otimes L_{B}\) to be \(\left(\omega, L_{A} \otimes L_{B}(\omega)\right)\). Now let \(I_{A}: H_{A} \rightarrow H_{A}\) and \(I_{B}: H_{B} \rightarrow H_{B}\) be the identity maps on \(H_{A}\) and \(H_{B}\) respectively. Suppose \(L=\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & \\ & -1\end{array}\right]\). Show that if \(\omega\) is non-entangled, say \(\omega=\boldsymbol{v} \otimes \boldsymbol{w}\), then \(\left(\omega, L \otimes I_{B}(\omega)\right)\) and \(\left(\omega, I_{A} \otimes L(\omega)\right)\) could be any pair of real numbers. (Here \(L_{A} \otimes I_{B}\) means that we perform observation \(L_{A}\) on \(A\) while we do not observe \(B\) at all. As we can see here, the obervation of only \(A\) and the observation of only \(B\) has no relation to each other.)
  4. Suppose \(L=\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & \\ & -1\end{array}\right]\), and let \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\) be real numbers such that \(a^{2}+b^{2}=1\), then calculate \((\omega, L \otimes\) \(\left.I_{B}(\omega)\right)\) and \(\left(\omega, I_{A} \otimes L(\omega)\right)\) when \(\omega=a \boldsymbol{e}_{1} \otimes \boldsymbol{e}_{1}+b \boldsymbol{e}_{2} \otimes \boldsymbol{e}_{2}\). (This is an entangled-state, where observing \(A\) is identical to observing B.)
